Nat.

This is a class assignment from my branding class, where the objective was to redesign an existing museum. I was given the Harvard Museum of Natural History, and I began the project by researching various aspects of the current museum.

I began the rebranding process by creating mind maps, performing logo studies, exploring word associations, and constructing mood boards, all of which can be seen in detail by clicking the above thumbnails. Then, I studied the existing brand to identify its weaknesses. Firstly, I noticed the name was wordy. Through naming exercises, I managed to shorten the name and produce a much more catchy alternative: The Nat. Around 35,000 children visit the museum every year, but the current branding, deeply rooted in Harvard traditions, does not reach this audience, which makes up about 15% of all visitors. The new brand aims to appeal to the young visitors by encouraging curiosity and appreciation in the natural world.

The logo attempts to portray a big, exciting world all condensed into a small dot. The illustrated world, called the Nat Dot, encourages people to look closely into something, however small, to reveal the exciting secrets within. The new logo is bold and simple, and it speaks to all age groups.

I also researched about the appropriate uses of punctuation marks in logos. I wondered what Nat comma could mean as opposed to the standard Nat period. After having played with this thought for a while, I determined the following uses for punctuation marks: commas for sublogos, exclamation marks for promotional events and advertisements, and quotation marks for pieces such as lectures and twitter pictures.

Big, Mini World—interative installation

The idea for Big, Mini World came naturally from branding the Nat. As a natural history museum, the Nat explores the entirety of Earth’s residues. In that sense, the Nat is a frozen microcosm of this planet. By taking an everyday object and blowing it up to a world of its own, the Big, Mini World explores the opposite.

As a permanent installation in the museum, it encourages all the visitors to bring their own sample of their world. It could be anything from leaves to a dustball in their desk drawers. With the help of an assistant, the visitor will receive a specimen inside a glass slide, which will be placed under the Nat Microscope. In a matter of seconds, they will experience a world of a vast smallness.
